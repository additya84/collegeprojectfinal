What is Percentage?

Percentage means "per hundred". The word percentage comes from the Latin word "per centum" which means out of one hundred.

Percentage simply means "out of 100."It uses the symbol % to show a share or a fraction of a whole. To understand it easily as a beginner, think of it this way: Simple Example: If you have 100 chocolates and you give 20 to a friend, you gave them 20% of your chocolates.The Benchmarks:100% means the absolute total or everything.50% means exactly half of something.25% means one-quarter (half of a half).

Formula Sheet

Percentage = (Value / Total Value) × 100
Value = Percentage × Total Value / 100
Total Value = Value × 100 / Percentage
Percentage Increase = ((New - Old) / Old) × 100
Percentage Decrease = ((Old - New) / Old) × 100
Successive Percentage Change = A + B + AB / 100
Fraction to Percentage: Fraction × 100
Decimal to Percentage: Decimal × 100
Commutative Property: x% of y = y% of x
Net Percentage Change: [a + b + (ab ÷ 100)]% (Use + for increase/profit and - for decrease/discount)
Equal Increase & Decrease: Net Loss = [x² ÷ 100]% (When a value increases by x% and then decreases by x%)
Price Increases by R%: Consumption Reduction = [R ÷ (100 + R)] × 100 %
Price Decreases by R%: Consumption Increase = [R ÷ (100 - R)] × 100 %
comparison formula If A is R% More than B: B is less than A by = [R ÷ (100 + R)] × 100 %
comparison Formula If A is R% Less than B: B is more than A by = [R ÷ (100 - R)] × 100 %

Shortcut Tricks

  • 1. The "Base 100" TrickNever use x variables. Assume the starting value is 100 to make calculation instant.
    Example: If a number increases by 20%, it becomes 120. If it then decreases by 20%, calculate 20% of 120 (24) and subtract it (120 - 24 = 96).
    Net change is a 4% decrease.
  • 2.Splitting Numbers TrickBreak down complex percentages into easy parts like 10%, 5%, and 1% using mental math.
    To find 10%: Move the decimal point one place to the left.To find 1%: Move the decimal point two places to the left.
    Example:Find 16% of 450.
    10% = 45
    5% = 22.5
    1% = 4.5
    (45 + 22.5 + 4.5 = 72)
  • 3. Digit Swap Trick (x% ofy = y% of x)
    If a percentage is too hard to calculate, flip the numbers around.
    Example: Find 64% of 50.
    Flip it: Find 50% of 64.
    Answer is instantly 32.
  • 4. Fractional Scale-Down Rule (1/x ->1 /x+1)
    If product expenditure must remain unchanged(A X B = Constant):
    An increase of 1/x requires a decrease of 1/x+1.
    Example If sugar prices rise by 25% 1/4, you must cut consumption by 1/4+1 = 1/5,
    which is exactly 20%.
  • 4. Fractional Scale-Up Rule (1/x ->1 /x-1)
    If product expenditure must remain unchanged(A X B = Constant):
    An increase of 1/x requires a decrease of 1/x-1.
    Example If sugar prices rise by 25% 1/4, you must cut consumption by 1/4-1 = 1/4,
    which is exactly 25%.
  • 6. The x^2 / 100 Absolute Loss TrickWhen a value increases and decreases by the exact same percentage (x%), there is always a net decrease.
    Shortcut: Net Loss = x^2/100% ExampleSalary goes up by 30% and then down by 30%. Net loss is 30^2/100 = 900/100 = 9% decrease
  • 7. Zero Elimination TrickWhen finding percentages of numbers that end in zero, multiply the non-zero digits and drop two zeros.
    Example: Find 40% of 70.
    Multiply4*7=28.

Question 3

1080 chocolates were distributed equally among students in such a way that the number of chocolates received by each student is 30% of the total number of students. How many chocolates did each student receive?

Question 3 Shortcut Solution
Let the number of chocolates per student be C.
Shortcut formula: C² = Total Chocolates x Percentage
C2 = 1080 x 30/100 = 1080 x 0.3 = 324
C = √324 = 18
.. The correct answer is 18.

Question 4

Share of clothes in monthly income = (2 11) x 55% = 10%.
